\section{Differential Pair Current–Voltage Characteristics}
	In this experiment, we set up the circuit in three different ways to measure $I_{out 1}$, $I_{out 2}$, and $V$ (common-source node voltage) values of the differential pair circuit. This arrangement was selected due to limitations with the equipment that we were using to take the data, the SMU has only two channels to get data from and we needed to get data from 3 places which needs 4 channels to take all of the data at once. For this experiment, we used one channel to vary the differential mode voltage into the circuit and used the other channel to measure the other three values one at a time. Afterwards, we changed the reference voltage for $V_2$ and reran all of the measurements. We used 3 reference voltages for $V_2$ which were 2.5 V, 3.5 V, and 4.5 V. After that, we changed the bias voltage for the circuit from 0.6 V to 1.5 V to take all of the measurements in strong inversion instead of weak inversion. \subsection{Analysis}

The nMOS differential pair was characterized for two bias conditions: near threshold ($V_b = 0.6$ V) and above threshold ($V_b = 1.5$ V). 
At $V_b = 0.6$ V (Figure \ref{fig:plot_iv_low}), the currents $I_1$ and $I_2$ depend primarily on the differential input $V_1 - V_2$. The curves for different $V_2$ values overlap closely, indicating a high common-mode rejection. The total current $I_1 + I_2$ remains approximately constant at $1.65\,\mu\text{A}$, consistent with a stable tail current.
The common-source node voltage $V$ (Figure \ref{fig:plot_vsource}) remains roughly constant when $V_1 < V_2$ (following the constant $V_2$) and increases linearly with $V_1$ once $V_1 > V_2$, as the source node tracks the gate of the active transistor to maintain a constant $V_{gs}$.
The differential-mode transconductance was extracted from the slope of $I_1 - I_2$ near $V_1 = V_2$:
\[
G_{dm} = \left.\frac{\partial (I_1 - I_2)}{\partial (V_1 - V_2)}\right|_{V_1 = V_2}
\]
For $V_b = 0.6$ V, $G_{dm} \approx 16\,\mu\mho$. As shown by the nearly identical slopes in Figure \ref{fig:plot_gdm}, this value of differential-mode transconductance gain does not change significantly as $V_2$ changes. At $V_b = 1.5$ V (Figure \ref{fig:plot_iv_high}), the tail current increases to approximately $307\,\mu\text{A}$, resulting in a significantly higher $G_{dm}$ of about $0.49\,\text{m}\mho$ and a wider linear input range.
\subsection{Discussion}

The results confirm that the differential pair responds mostly to $V_1 - V_2$, with little dependence on common-mode voltage. This is supported by the overlap of $I_1 - I_2$ curves across different $V_2$ values and the nearly constant $I_1 + I_2$.
Small changes in $I_1 + I_2$ at large input differences likely occur when the bias transistor leaves saturation or the input devices enter the ohmic region.
Increasing $V_b$ from near-threshold to strong inversion increases both the tail current and $G_{dm}$, while also expanding the linear range. However, this comes at the cost of higher power consumption and reduced allowable common-mode range due to larger required $V_{gs}$.
