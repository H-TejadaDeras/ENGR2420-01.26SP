\subsection{Results}

     \begin{figure}[H]

    \caption{Voltage transfer characteristics of the differential amplifier biased in strong inversion at $V_2 = 2.5\text{ V}$, $3.5\text{ V}$, and $4.5\text{ V}$.}\label{fig:exp1_SI}
    \end{figure}

    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{../diagrams/lab6_exp1_WI.pdf}
    \caption{Voltage transfer characteristics of the differential amplifier biased in weak/moderate inversion at $V_2 = 2.5\text{ V}$, $3.5\text{ V}$, and $4.5\text{ V}$.}\label{fig:exp1_WI}
    \end{figure}

    \subsection{Analysis}
    The measured voltage transfer characteristics in both strong inversion and weak/moderate inversion show
	four distinct operating regions as $V_1$ is swept from $0\text{ V}$ to $5\text{ V}$. 
	Initially, $V_{out}$ remains at around $0\text{ V}$. 
	As $V_1$ increases past the bias threshold, the output voltage begins to rise, proportional to $\kappa(V_1 - V_b)$. 
	As $V_1$ approaches the constant reference voltage $V_2$, the circuit enters its high-gain region,
	characterized by a steep slope where a small change in $V_1$ produces a large change in $V_{out}$. 
	Finally, when $V_1$ becomes greater than $V_2$, the output saturates and flattens out near the positive supply rail, $V_{dd}$.
    
    \subsection{Discussion}
    Comparing the two VTC plots shows a difference in behavior depending on the biasing regime. 
	When biased in weak/moderate inversion, the transition through the high-gain region 
	($V_1 \approx V_2$) is noticeably sharper and more abrupt than in strong inversion. 
	In strong inversion, this transition is smoother and spans a slightly wider range of input voltages. 
	The plots show that the differential amplifier cannot pull its output voltage completely down to ground once it enters the active region. 
	This limitation is caused by the tail current bias transistor ($M_b$) requiring a minimum voltage drop to remain saturated, thus imposing a lower limit on $V_{out}$ of approximately $\kappa(V_1 - V_b)$.

    \label{fig:exp2_gm}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Analysis}
In this experiment, the differential-mode voltage gain ($A_{dm}$) was determined using two different methods. 
First, by sweeping $V_1$ around $V_2$ and fitting a line to the steepest section of the voltage 
transfer characteristic, a gain of 215.02 was determined. 
Next, the parameters of the amplifier's gain equation—incremental output resistance 
($r_o$) and transconductance ($g_m$) were measured independently. 
Setting $V_{dm} = 0 \text{ V}$ and sweeping $V_{out}$ gave an I-V curve 
where the inverse of the shallow slope gave an output resistance $r_o = 7.33 \text{ M}\Omega$. 
Finally, holding the output voltage as a constant and sweeping the differential 
input voltage gave a curve where the slope near $V_{dm} = 0 \text{ V}$ gave a transconductance
 $g_m = 61.43~\mu\mho$.

\subsection{Discussion}
The theoretical incremental differential-mode voltage gain can be found
using the relationship $A_{dm} = R_{out} \cdot G_m$. 
Multiplying the output resistance ($7.33 \text{ M}\Omega$) 
by the transconductance ($61.43~\mu\mho$) gives a calculated gain of approximately 450.3. 
This computed value is close to the 215.02 gain measured from the slope of the voltage transfer 
characteristic. The slight error of about 6\% is expected 
and could be caused by minor variations in the 
exact operating points.

\section{Unity-Gain Follower}
\subsection{Results}
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{../diagrams/lab6_exp3_diagram.pdf}
    \caption{Schematic diagram of the differential amplifier as a unity-gain follower.}\label{fig:exp3}
    \end{figure}

    
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{../diagrams/lab6_exp3_unity_gain.pdf}
    \caption{Voltage transfer characteristic of the unity-gain follower configuration, showing a measured incremental gain of 0.985.}\label{fig:exp3_UG}
    \end{figure}

	\subsection{Analysis}
	By connecting the output to the inverting input terminal, the differential 
	amplifier was turned into a unity-gain follower. Sweeping $V_{in}$ from rail 
	to rail created a linear relationship between the input and 
	output voltages over most of the operating range. 
	To estimate the incremental gain, a line of best fit was applied to the linear 
	portion of the measured data, excluding the regions near the voltage rails 
	where clipping occurs. The resulting slope of the best fit line gives a 
	measured gain of $0.985$. 
	Noticeable clipping occurs near the lower bound of the sweep (below 
	$\approx 0.3\,\text{V}$) and near the upper bound (above $\approx 4.5\,\text{V}$), 
	where the output voltage begins to differ from the linear trend.

	\subsection{Discussion}
	The measured incremental gain of $0.985$ indicates that the circuit functions 
	pretty effectively as a voltage follower, since the gain is close to the ideal value 
	of $1$. The small difference from unity gain is caused by the finite open-loop 
	gain of the differential amplifier. Because the open loop gain is not infinite, 
	the closed loop gain of the follower cannot be exactly one.
	The clipping observed near the voltage rails reflects the compliance limits of 
	the amplifier. When the input voltage is near ground or $V_{dd}$, the 
	transistors leave the saturation region required for proper operation. 
	As a result, the amplifier can no longer track the input voltage accurate;y, 
	causing to the observed difference from linear behavior near the extremes of 
	the sweep.
